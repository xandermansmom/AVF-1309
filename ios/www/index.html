Perks

Less obvious perks of using 'live' data resources include logins and payments. How could Twitter be used in these two scenarios?

Some perks of using Twitter login with your app is explained in Twitter‚Äôs developer documentation at https://dev.twitter.com/docs/auth/sign-twitter.

Logins

  • Ease of use - A new visitor to your site only has to click two buttons in order to sign in for the first time.
  • Twitter integration - The Sign in with Twitter flow can grant authorization to use Twitter APIs on your users behalf.
  • OAuth based (Open Authorization) - A wealth of client libraries and example code are compatible with Sign in with Twitter's API.

Payments

An example of how Twitter is already being used for payments is a startup called Chirpify.

Chirpify allows a brand with a following on Twitter to connect its PayPal business account to them and then tweet items for sale through a simple dashboard. This could include time-sensitive deals or highlighted items that are on sale or need to be moved. When the user replies with a tweet saying they want to buy the product, the money gets deducted from their PayPal account using OAuth permission. If the buyer doesn‚Äôt have a PayPal account, they are prompted to set one up and connect it to Chirpify.

Accessibility

What are some of the ways that you could change your HTML on your Demo app to be more accessible? (Remember, you can use the Section tag with JQuery, for instance)

Since Full Sail has always taught us how to build our apps to meet accessibility criteria, I don't really see anything that could improve my app's accessibility. I've always provided alt text with my images, used well-positioned labels on my form fields, etc.

Visit a video service on the web (like YouTube) and search for "Screen Reader Demo" and "Mobile Screen Reader" - what are some of the common frustrations (or common support mechanisms) that you see among the examples that are given - how could you fix them (or use them)?

Some issues or frustrations that arise using a screen reader are:

  1. No alt text for pictures
  2. Nondescriptive alt text
  3. No skip navigation
  4. Poorly designed/unlabeled form
  5. Mouse required for navigation
  6. Page layout causing confusing screen reader feedback
  7. Pop-up frustration
  8. table won’t read linearly
  9. Screen reader crash
  10. Conflict between screen reader and application

Most of these issues can be fixed by just keeping a handy list of what not to do like for example (no alt text for images) just be sure to include alt text for your images and make sure the alt text is descriptive enough. The rest is pretty much the same. Being aware of possible issues that can arise and avoiding it.

Devices: Please list two devices (and some of their features) that are made specifically to support Accessibility. iOS 5 has some accessibility features such as the following:

VoiceOver - a system that allows people with visual impairments to navigate the touchscreen of an iOS device. Switch it on and your iOS device speaks to you, telling you what’s under your fingers as you touch areas of the device.

Zoom - Enable it and you can zoom the screen by double tapping with three fingers. To zoom out, use this same gesture. To move around a zoomed screen, drag three fingers.

Large Text – Enlarges text for your app.

AssistiveTouch - Tap it, and a gray overlay window appear from which you can select Gestures, Device, Home, and Favorites icons.

Android has some of the following accessibility features:

Text-to-speech - synthesizes speech from text for immediate playback or to create a sound file.

Haptic feedback – a tacile feedback technology which takes advantage of the sense of touch by applying forces, vibrations, or motions to the user.

Gesture navigation - gestures allow users to interact with your app by manipulating the screen objects you provide.

Trackball and directional-pad navigation – clickable trackball that users can move in any director. A clickable d-pad that allows users to navigate in four directions.

Android developers can also build their own accessibility services, which can provide enhanced usability features such as:

  • Audio prompting
  • Physical feedback
  • Alternative navigation modes

Mobile Apps: Please list two apps (either Android or iOS, or both) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

Mobile Accessibility is a screen-access application which allows people who are blind or have low vision to use an Android phone in an intuitive, easy and simple way.

VoiceOver is a gesture-based screen reader that lets you enjoy the fun and simplicity of iOS even if you can’t see the screen.

Industry Research

What are the 'top selling' app categories in the U.S.? Japan? Great Britain?

In the U.S., the top selling categories are dependent on who is using the app and what type of device they are using. For moms, the most popular categories are games, then social networking, fitness apps and then personal assistant apps like shopping tools.

However, the most popular apps for young adults are lifestyle apps like Sports, Health and Fitness then multimedia apps like music, media and entertainment. Due to the fact that Japan’s exclusive iPhone provider, SoftBank Mobile, does not release stats or official data, finding something more current was difficult.

However, according to my research, the top selling apps in Japan are first and foremost, games, and then second, the productivity apps. The top selling app in London right now fits the personal assistant category. It is called Hailo and is a mobile app that enables the user to order a licensed cab and bill your credit car at the taxi door. However, games are also on the top rated mobile app category in the U.K.

Based on your three answers above, what might you infer about each of the markets listed?

Based on these answers, the categories of preference for each country is dependent on the lifestyles of the people involved.

For instance, in the U.S., the popular apps vary dependent on whether the user is a mom, or a single young adult. Moms chose games, social networking, fitness apps and then personal assistant apps. This tells me they like to take micro-breaks in between their busy parenting schedule, they like to stay in shape, and they like apps that help them take care of their day-to-day routines like shopping. However, young adults like apps that help them stay in shape and apps that provide entertainment.

Japan’s number one choice is for gaming apps but their second choice is productivity apps. This tells me, they are a very productive country that likes to play games when they take micro-breaks in between their workdays.

Britain’s most popular app at the moment is a personal assistant app called Hailo. These types of apps are timesaving apps that tell me Londoners value their time but in between times of productivity, they also choose game apps for micro-breaks.
